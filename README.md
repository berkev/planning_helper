# Planning Tools

This is a collection of schedulers, lists and calenders with tooling around it to support my daily activities and long term planning.

## What is the purpose?

I already use it all. Digital calendars on my phone, physical ones hanging on my bedroom door. Lists and notes, to-dos and long term plans everywhere.
There is just to much clutter, too much fragmented information. Two semesters ago I used an excel table to map out my learning times for each course in uni. This semester 
I used a lot of gitlab for software engineering and was intrigued by the planning tools so I created milestones and issues out of my assignments and exam dates.
I tried different approaches but nothing quite sticked with me. Maybe I just have to pick one and stay disciplined, but that is boring to me. 

Another way could be this: I create a database of everything I might want to achieve, get done or don't want to forget. One single source of truth for all of my todos, plans 
and goals. Then, create different tools around this database: 

- Simple Visualisers like lists
- Analyzing tools
- Schedulers that use different scheduling policies for arranging the tasks
- Creation mechanisms for these items, like ical appointment import
- AI-integration, for generating roadmaps towards goals

I am sure this list will extend with time moving.

## What do I want to keep track of?

Like previously mentioned, I am  interested in to-dos, like daily/weekly/monthly/annual tasks. Then next to tasks I want to keep track of my long term responsibilites, like uni work.
Best case is that these can be broken down into smaller pieces to eventually get tasks out of them. 


## What are tasks anyways?

I should clarify what I mean with these words. Tasks, goals, responsibilities are general ideas that different people (or even me at different points in time) might have differing definitions for.

The task: In this context a task is something I want to get done in one sitting (or standing, kneeling, depends on the thing I do). Examples might be: Vacuum the home, take out the trash, do the home excercise for my course on operating systems. 
A task is generally something that I just want to get done and not care too much about perfectionism. It should be something small enough to get started without overthinking it.

The responsibility: This is something I will overthink about. Something that I want to get done, and be as good as can be. This might be a group project or an exam, a contract or a contest. The main attributes of a responsibility are that it is terminated and it has a measurable outcome. Measurability might have vastly differing forms due to the nature of the respo.    

The skill: A skill is another long term item for my planning, but with a generally lower priority compared to responsibilities. It shares the "working towards" nature of the responsibility, but lacks a clear terminating date. 
A skill is something I consider measurable, even if just by comparing with my past self. I would say a skill is a latent (indirectly masurable) thing. I can not really say how good I am at "programming" but I can make a list of all concepts surrounding programming and count the items I already understand or can implement. I can not say how good I am at calisthenics, but I can count the seconds I can hold a planche. 

Both the skill and the responsibility are big things, but the responsibility ends while the skill stays. Working on skills can increase my ability to perform tasks efficiently and with high quality.

## Traits of the todos

Two concepts serve to distinguish the upper mentioned items: Measurability and termination.


|     traits         |         Measurable               |               Not Measurable          |
|--------------------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
|   Terminated       |     written exam                 |          Doing the taxes              | 
|  Not Terminated    |     learning languages           |         cleaning the kitchen          |


Of course you could argue that "doing the taxes" might be measurable by the amount of money I can save or that "cleaning the kitchen" is something that
could be very well terminated by a schedule. This example is how I see these things for my practical life.

What I want to concentrate on is the abstraction I can get by having these traits for a task: instead of managing tasks related to 
household, exam preparation and learning vocabulary as seperate things, I can have a table of all my long and short term items and simply note
for every one of the if they are measurable or terminated, both or neither of them.  

Keeping track of terminated tasks makes it easier to design schedules for finishing them, while other tasks might be done in downtime.
